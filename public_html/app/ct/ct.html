improving the employee experience

Every successful digital company needs to foster a culture of empowerment, innovation and trust. This is the story of how I helped GE create an internal shared IT services website which empowered employees to create the solutions that helped transform GE into a Digital Industrial company.

MY ROLE

I was approached by the shared IT services team, aka CoreTech, to conduct user testing on the beta version of their shared IT services catalog site. Based on the results of the first round of testing my engagement in this project expanded to include creating an alternative design, conducting A/B testing and helping to set the overall design direction.

EXPERIENCE STRATEGY & VISION

I created an alternative design based on the results of user testing I conducted on CoreTech’s beta design. Through these artifacts I was able to share with the CoreTech team a visualization of the users needs and conduct A/B testing.

USER TESTING

Using personas developed by the CoreTech team I created a user testing kit where I asked participants to solve a series of scenarios based on the persona they most closely reflected. After translating these results into takeaways for CoreTech I was able create suggestions on how they could improve their experience.

PLANNING & DEVELOPMENT STRATEGY

Working with the client, CoreTech, I advocated for the user based on the results of my testing to ensure user’s needs would be met and balanced those needs with business requirements. Through the results of my testing I was able to provide valuable guidance which helped the CoreTech prioritize their roadmap in order to meet their user’s highest impact needs first.

the challenge

EMPOWER EMPLOYEES TO CREATE NEXT GENERATION INDUSTRIAL SOLUTIONS

My challenge was to help CoreTech create a solution that would streamline the process of acquiring IT services in GE and empower GE employees to make product and service acquisition decisions on their own. In order to do this I needed to understand how the process had traditionally worked and to empathize with users in a way that I could truly understand their experience when interacting with IT shared services.

That interaction was a very complex and time intensive process that required a lot of “tribal knowledge” in order to complete. IT shared services was spread out across many different portals which utilized different processes and designs. Additionally there were very few products and services that could actually be obtained without requiring some form of person-to-person interaction. Even more challenging for employees was understanding how each of their products and services related to one another and how those dependences would affect the solution they were trying to build.

the approach

CONSULTANT TO SOMETHING MORE

I was originally only brought into this project to conduct a single round of usability testing on the beta version of CoreTech’s shared IT services site. However, the results of that round of testing suggested that substantial changes needed to be made to the information architecture strategy and the goals the project was striving to achieve.

Based on my recommendations the CoreTech team agreed to my proposal to improve their own Beta design while I explored a different direction for the project with a mockup created in Axure. Once both were ready I would conduct a second round of usability testing to evaluate if 1) The changes to the beta design were successful in addressing the shortcomings identified in the first round of testing and 2) if an alternative direction, as represented in my mockup, would more effectively meet users needs.

OPEN COMMUNICATION

Building an alternative design meant becoming more than just a Usability Testing Consultant. I was potentially producing a design that would drastically change the direction of already well established project should my alternative direction prove to be far more effective than the current approach. As a result it became imperative that I maintained a high level of transparency and communication around the work that I was doing in order to create a strong working dynamic.

BUILDING TRUST AND AWARENESS

UX testing, research and design is a fairly new addition within GE, so any project that I was involved with while at GE always included a lot of evangelizing. That was particularly true during my time with CoreTech as it was a very politically charged project with a lot of stakeholders who had a personal stake in the project’s outcome. As a result my goal became to keep the focus of all the stakeholders on the user’s and what they would need in order to be successful when interacting with CoreTech.

Thankfully I already had years of experience working within GE’s culture which I could use in order to communicate the results of my research in a way which stakeholders could relate too. The legacy culture at GE was still very focused on quantitative results and while UX does not always yield very clear cut percentage-based results I did my best to represent my results in terms of a percentage of users who were successful at meeting the goals all of the stakeholders had agreed on for the project.

the DISCOVERY

DISCONNECTED VISION

Discovery research was run by an external contractor who conducted research interviews with a collection of different employees at GE and then created detailed user personas based on those results. I was given the results of their research and a copy of their personas to use as my discovery for my portion of this project as well.

The personas and research results were very well done and provided great insight into the needs, motivations and pain-points of CoreTech’s audience. As a matter of fact I was surprised to see how much of a disconnect between the insights shown in the personas and the actual design there was. It appeared as the though the design had been created without taking any guidance from the researched conducted.

At a high level, the research showed that the audience had three shared goals 1) to have a self-service shared IT services site 2) to have one unified location for all shared IT services information and 3) to be able to see the roadmap for products and services.

A MOVING TARGET

While conducting Discovery there were two major challenges I had to overcome. The first was that we didn't have enough time for a true Design Sprint, so Discovery had to be done on the fly each sprint. The second was that we were actually designing ahead of the client. That is to say we were designing a system to handle data that actually didn't exist yet.

This ended up proving to be the most difficult challenge as we were never able to get a clear picture of the type of data would be once it did exist. To compensate for this I worked with the client to create design outcomes that they agreed to for each of our personas. Working toward achieving these agreed upon outcomes helped the entire team understand the direction the project should be heading in each sprint. We constantly referred to these outcomes to drive functionality, direction and prioritization conversations.

To help the team prioritize we worked with the client to identify many different personas who would be relying on our application in one way or another. After we created the different personas we were able to prioritize who we would focus on for each epic. For the phase that I was involved in we focused mostly on Auggie and Tammy.

Algorithm Developer

As an Algorithm Developer I want to be able to identify the data I need to find the root cause of a AV error in less than two minutes so that I can decrease my development time.

Fleet Manager

As a Fleet Manager I want to be able to see where each AV is, what state it is in and who is driving it so that I can manage testing routes and test results.

Testing Specialist

As a Testing Specialist I want to know what routes will be run each day and who will be on them so that I can prioritize my test cases.

PREDICTING FUTURE USE

Since we were building a solution to solve a problem that wasn't fully defined yet, due to the lack of data, I had to get a bit creative to understand what the users anticipated they would need from the application in order to be successful. In order to help users visualize how they would interact with the application I created Storyboards for each of the different personas depicting them using the application to reach that persona's top one or two goals.

Using these Storyboards as a talking point in user interviews I was able to judge the accuracy of our current design direction and get feedback on our vision very early on.

THE REQUIREMENTS

GETTING THE RIGHT INFORMATION - FAST

In every aspect of my research one thing was consistently emphasized, speed and not just in a technical sense, but also from a information architecture perspective. Auggie needed to be able to quickly find the root cause of a specific issue and then see if it had been repeated in other cases. Frank needed to quickly find out who was driving what route and what state their AV was in. Dan needed to know the results of a test in order to track the program's success over time in order to prioritize the roadmap.

Focusing on these motivations when approaching the design for our solution helped to give me the perspective I needed to make any users interaction, quick and effective. To help myself build this perspective further I composed a set of user stories for each persona based on my research findings.

the FRAMEWORK

REDUCING AMBIGUITY

The Storyboards helped me to get a firm understanding of the functionality that users would need in order to successfully meet their goals, next came the challenge of designing the experience. In order to get user feedback early and iterate quickly I choose to create low fidelity mockups using Axure and conduct Usability Testing with those mockups.

Using Axure gave me the ability to create a new version and test them every couple of days, which meant I was able to keep ahead of a fast moving development team and provide insight to the project management team as they prioritized each sprint.

ITERATIVE DESIGN, GRAND VISION

Having validated Storyboard, Personas and User Stories meant that the client, the development team and myself all had an agreed upon shared vision for our application. This meant that I could iterate my designs each sprint to test out future functionality in support of that vision with relative ease.

The result was a quick moving, but validated approach to designing a complex technical solution within the confines of a tight timeline. I was proud of this approach because it did not sacrifice user feedback for the benefits of speed.

FINAL DESIGN

INTRODUCING ... A BETTER WAY

SIMPLE AND FAMILAR

At its core, this application is a search engine and what do users expect when they visit a search engine? Google. Keeping with that convention I designed a simple, but powerful homepage that allows for easy access to personalization and advanced features, but most importantly allows users to start searching quickly.

DATA ARCHITECTURE TO SUPPORT THE WAY YOU WORK

I designed the Advanced Search page to reflect the way algorithm developers model searches. The Advanced Search page let's users build their query by "and-ing", "or-ing" or "not-ing" together a series of search tags. Favorited queries can be accessed quickly from the users profile and an estimated search result count is displayed to improve query result accuracy.

MAKING INSIGHTS EASIER

Algorithm Developers like Auggie spent the majority of their time debugging issues by manually finding trends across multiple, similar data records. I designed the results page to help identify these trends and other records of interest faster with a dashboard view of the queries results and metadata of the resulting records.

COMMAND CENTER

I wanted to create a live tracking page that was both functional and a morale booster. This dashboard view lets users focus on a single car/driver, viewing it's status history and detailed information or see the location of the entire fleet in real-time.

THE IMPACT

ONE SMALL STEP ...

This project was short, but in that time we managed to accomplish a great deal. Using an iterative design-thinking approach we were able to unify the client, development team and project management around a single vision for the future of this application as the functionality behind it becomes available. By focusing on the users and their unmet needs we were able to create an application that increases working efficiency and morale.

At the same time we were able to make sure we never took the user out of user experience. Using a rigorous testing cycle we were able to confidently deliver a product for a client that for which we had validation it would meet their users needs. This project was fast and furious, but very successful from start to finish.
