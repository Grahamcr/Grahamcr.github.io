THE ALL SEEING PONYAK

One of the biggest challenges of running a technology consultancy is managing employees as projects are won, lost, start and end. This is the story of how I designed an application to help leadership create different employee assignment scenarios to predict how winning or losing projects would affect the workforce.

To comply with my confidentiality agreement I have omitted confidential information. Some designs are reinterpretations of the original

MY ROLE

To help a technology consultancy company prepare for multiple possible contract win/loss scenarios I was asked to digitize a process they were currently doing with long-running sticky notes. To discover the most effective design I ran very rapid design and test iterations where I tested a new design with users every one to two days over the course of 10 days.

MIND MAPPING

This project had to be completed on an extremely tight timeline of less than three weeks, so there was little time to run a proper discovery sprint. Therefore I ran a mind mapping session with the project stakeholders to get a understanding the challenge, audience and business requirements.

RAPID PROTOTYPING

Since there was little time for discovery it was critical to maintain even more frequent than normal user feedback during the design process. To facilitate this I took a rapid prototyping where I produced a new mockup or A/B test every couple of days based on the usability results from the previous iteration.

A/B TESTING

Getting the Information Architecture right was very important to the client. To test out different approaches to structuring the data I designed a couple different multivariate A/B tests to determine which approach would be the most intuitive to users.

HIGH FIDELITY DESIGN & STYLE GUIDE

Once a design direction had been settled on based on the results of the Rapid Prototyping and User Testing I created a High Fidelity Click-Through Mockup using Axure which showed all the expected interactions, tone and aesthetics.

the challenge

A High Impact Technology Team

A technology consultancy company approached me to help them improve the design of an application they were working on to digitize possible workforce allocation scenarios based on the projects they had bids out for and were currently working. The timeframe to complete this design was very short, only three weeks, as the developers who would be working on implementing the design were doing the work as a bench project.

The goal was to create an application that would allow leadership to both view how the company's workforce was currently allocated and also create plans to allocate the workforce in differently to account for different possible scenarios, such as winning a new large contract. In the future the stakeholders also wanted to add intelligence to the solution so that it could use metadata about employees, such as their home address, previous projects and skill levels to suggest the optimal allocation of employees given a specific scenario.

the discovery

THE WAR-ROOM APPROACH

Three weeks to develop a complete design and style guide for a complex application was not a lot of time. Therefore instead of running a dedicated discovery sprint, I opted for a rapid prototyping approach were the first iteration would be informed by the results of a Mind Mapping session.

I was able to get all of the stakeholders and some leadership to join together for an afternoon that we spent talking through the user's needs, motivations and goals, as well as, the business requirements that would have to be met. The result was an artifact that provided insight into the application’s audience and the functionality that would be required in order for the design to be successful.

the test

OPTIMIZING CONTENT FOR COMPREHENSION

For this application to be a success it had to be faster to use than manually manipulating sticky notes on a whiteboard and taking pictures of different employee/team configurations. This is a surprisingly high bar to overcome as users can quickly modify sticky notes and the barrier of taking a picture isn’t actually that high. Therefore, I needed to make sure that I was able to present content and functionality in a way that was highly intuitive.

To discover the perfect information architecture I conducted multivariate A/B tests like the one below that tests a vertical vs. horizontal layout and list vs. tabbed scenario access. Multivariate tests rarely give results that point to one pure solution over the other so my next iteration was typically a combination of features from both designs.

THE FRAMEWORK

RAPID, ITERATIVE DISCOVERY THROUGH TESTING

My plan to compensate for the tight timeline was to put meaningful designs in front of the user and conduct scenario based usability tests as frequently as reasonably possible. This approach turned out to be very successful as it provided the user with real world scenarios to test the wireframes with and it gave myself ample opportunities to validate that the design was heading in the right direction.

FINAL DESIGN

INTRODUCING … PONYAK

The gallery below shows the final deep interaction mockup I created with Axure

CLEAN, INTUITIVE, FAST

I designed the workspace to take advantage of many conventions and affordances in order to ensure that there would be a short learning curve to becoming proficient using PONYAK. From the bench full of draggable playing cards to the intuitive search and tab layout, PONYAK was designed for speed.

INFORMED DECISIONS

The goal of leadership was to make the best employee assignments possible, which means having the information they need in order to make informed decisions. Therefore PONYAK not only makes suggestions based on what it knows, but also shares the information used to make that suggestion with the user so they can make a determination for themselves.

FAST ACCESS TO ACTIONABLE INFORMATION

Clicking on any playing card brings up that employee’s details. I designed the presentation of these details to make actionable information easy to identify to empower user to quickly find the information they need to drive decisions.

COLLABORATIVE DECISION MAKING

This company’s leadership was spread across the region and country so it was important that they be able to collaborate on workforce allocation decisions. To facilitate this need I designed an annotation feature that allowed users to chat about a particular position on a team.

THE IMPACT

Success!

In the end I was able to design an application which was faster, more secure and more informative than the previous approach of keeping long running sticky notes on a whiteboard. I was excited to see how successful the design was, despite the tight schedule and limited discovery. Last I was in contact with the company, PONYAK was still under development, but preparing for an internal beta release within a couple of weeks.
